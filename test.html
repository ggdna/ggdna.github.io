Mutual information has been used to reason about lots of complex systems -- flocks of birds, artificial neural networks, developing embryos, and more. But estimating mutual information is hard, especially when working with high-dimensional data. This is because of the curse of dimensionality. Here, we try to learn low-dimensional representations of high-dimensional data which can be used to approximate MI, ``cheating'' the curse. We use the approximation to study a couple real problems in biology.

We came up with some tricks for easily designing large DNA barcode libraries. You can use these tricks through a simple python package (`pip install seqwalk`).

Biological systems compute very energy efficiently, especially when compared to today's silicon chips. In this work, we thought about architectures for chemical computers that could be much more energy efficient than traditional silicon ones. We came up with some abstract designs for feedforward digital logic circuits that use only physically reversible rearrangement reactions on a surface.
