More concretely, I've spent time thinking about chemical reaction networks, DNA barcodes, single cell measurements, and information measures.

quantifying information flow in single cell data (coming soon!)

It's clear that information flows through biological systems [Tkacik & Bialek, 2016]. Yet, quantitative descriptions of biological information flow from experimental data remain rare. One major reason is that estimating mutual information in high dimension is extremely hard. We introduce SIMI, a method for measuring informativity in high-dimension, then use SIMI to quantify information flow in a variety of single cell datasets.

de Bruijn graphs and DNA barcode design (in revision)

How many orthogonal DNA barcodes can you make given a certain barcode length? In this paper, we establish an equivalence between sequence symmetry minimization and finding Hamiltonian paths in de Bruijn graphs. This yields a theoretical framework for determining upper bounds on orthogonal library size, and some neat tricks for quickly designing very large barcode libraries, which we implement in a python package (`pip install seqwalk`).

reversible computing with chemical reaction networks on a surface

Biological systems compute very energy efficiently, especially when compared to today's silicon chips [Boahen, 2022]. In this work, we explored architectures for chemical computers that could be much more energy efficient than traditional silicon ones. We present designs for feedforward digital logic circuits that use only physically reversible rearrangement reactions on a surface.
